# Site Review: Ziva Wernick — Software Developer

**Site:** https://radiant-zivawernick.ziva-wernick.workers.dev/
**Reviewed:** 2026-04-05
**Perspective:** Potential client seeking a software/website developer

---

## First Impressions

The site loads on a Cloudflare Workers domain, which immediately tells me this is either a staging/preview version or hasn't been moved to a custom domain yet. If I stumbled on this as a cold lead, the URL `radiant-zivawernick.ziva-wernick.workers.dev` doesn't inspire the same confidence as a clean `zivawernick.dev` would. Minor thing, but first impressions matter when you're selling professionalism.

That said, the site itself is well-structured. The navigation is clear, and I can find what I need — services, projects, contact — without hunting. Good.

## What Works Well

**Clear positioning.** "Social Impact Software Developer" is a strong, differentiated identity. Most dev portfolios say "I build things." This one says "I build things *for a reason*." As a nonprofit or mission-driven org looking for a developer, I'd immediately feel like I'm in the right place.

**Accessibility is a headline feature, not an afterthought.** High contrast toggle, reduced motion toggle, dark mode, WCAG 2.2 compliance, skip-to-content, keyboard nav — this is walking the talk. If I'm an org that serves diverse populations, seeing this tells me Ziva actually cares about inclusion in practice, not just in copy.

**Solid tech stack.** React, Astro, Node, Python, Java, TypeScript — it's a real full-stack range. The inclusion of Wix Velo and no-code/low-code tools is smart for the target audience (nonprofits and small orgs that may need affordable, maintainable solutions).

**The CALMe project is compelling.** An offline-first PWA for trauma-informed mental health support? That's not a toy project — it shows real-world problem solving and empathy-driven engineering. As a potential client, this is the kind of portfolio piece that makes me think "this person understands *why* software matters, not just *how* to write it."

## Where I'd Want More (as a Client)

**Services are broad — maybe too broad.** There are 8 service categories listed, ranging from "Web Development" to "Community & Content" to "No Code / Low Code Solutions." As a potential client, I'd wonder: is this a developer, a consultant, a content strategist, or a Wix specialist? Breadth can read as "jack of all trades" if it isn't framed carefully. Suggestions:

- Group them into 2-3 tiers (e.g., "Build," "Optimize," "Maintain")
- Or lead with the 2-3 strongest offerings and let the rest be discoverable

**No pricing signals or engagement model.** I don't expect a rate card, but something like "Typical projects start at..." or "I work in 2-week sprints" or "Retainer and project-based options available" would help me self-qualify. Right now I have to reach out just to learn if we're even in the same ballpark.

**Testimonials are absent.** For someone with 5 years of experience and community leadership roles (Women in Tech Israel, Lime Connect), there should be at least 2-3 short quotes from past clients or collaborators. Social proof is the single highest-converting element on a freelancer's site.

**Project descriptions are thin.** The projects are listed with one-line descriptions. As a client, I want to know: What was the problem? What did you build? What was the outcome? Even 3-4 sentences per project in a case-study format would dramatically increase trust. "I built a PWA" is good. "The org needed offline access in crisis zones with unreliable connectivity, so I built an offline-first PWA using service workers and IndexedDB — it's now used by X people" is *much* better.

**The "About" section mixes personal narrative with professional credentials.** The Aliyah story and neurodivergent identity are meaningful and humanizing — but as a client scanning quickly, I'd want the professional credibility (5 years experience, current role at Jaffa Institute, community leadership) to hit me first, with personal details enriching the picture rather than competing for attention.

## Bottom Line

If I were a nonprofit or social-impact org looking for a developer, this site would get Ziva on my shortlist. The values alignment is clear, the technical range is real, and the accessibility commitment is genuinely impressive.

To move from "shortlist" to "let's talk," I'd want to see:

1. **2-3 client testimonials** (even short ones)
2. **Richer project case studies** with problem/solution/outcome
3. **A clearer service hierarchy** so I know what Ziva does *best*, not just everything Ziva *can* do
4. **Some signal of how engagements work** (timeline, process, pricing range)

Strong foundation. A few strategic additions would make this a genuinely compelling client-conversion tool, not just a nice portfolio.
